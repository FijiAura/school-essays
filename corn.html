Food Security – Corn

Ah; corn. It’s truly one of the vegetables grains of all time. In the year 2022 alone, over 1.16 BILLION tonnes of corn were produced. That’s 145kg per human on earth! This report discusses the food security of, well, corn.

Around 31.5% of corn is grown in the United States. China comes in 2nd, with 23.3%, then Brazil in 3rd, with 10%. Combined, those 3 countries make up around two-thirds of global production.


Find more statistics at Statista.

Those 3 countries grow the most for different reasons. The US produces the most due to its low population density, so most of its corn is used as animal feed — only about 13% is exported. Meanwhile, China and Brazil’s high populations mean more domestic use.

When thinking of corn, you’ll most likely think of sweet corn. So “145kg per human on earth” will seem outrageous; no one eats that much corn! However, most of it isn’t sweet corn; in fact, it’s processed into animal feed, ethanol, sweetener, and starch.

Corn grows best in climates with temperatures around 20-30 degrees. Now, you might notice that it only gets those temperatures for 3 months of the year,June (06), July (07), and August (08). You have to consider that there's lots of land in the area, allowing for lots of corn to grow. So its grown in that area due to the perfect mix of land and climate.

Challenges facing corn production in the US include:

  • Disease pressure (notably southern rust) spreading in parts of the Midwest and South.
  • Localized flooding, delayed emergence, or drought creating yield variability across states.
  • Rising input costs and tight margins increasing economic pressure.
  • Market volatility due to uncertain yields and large USDA estimates.
  • Late planting and heat stress during key growth stages.

(Please note: data is from 2008.)

Did you know many farmers can’t even replant their own corn? This is because of patented GMO seed lines like “Roundup Ready.” The company BAYER owns a patent for corn resistant to its herbicide, Roundup; meaning farmers replanting saved seeds could face patent infringement claims.
